## Bottom Line

The article is marginally relevant as it provides a concrete case study of the state's constitutive role in the tech economy, directly illustrating the theory's claim that tech dominance is a product of geopolitical strategy rather than a 'parcelized' usurpation of state sovereignty (C3). By detailing how US export controls and Chinese regulatory mandates actively shape the competitive landscape, supply chains, and investment priorities of AI chipmakers, the text serves as a strong illustrative example of the state's power to direct the tech sector. However, it remains 'marginal' rather than 'relevant' because it is an op-ed that lacks the systematic quantitative data (e.g., specific capex figures or R&D budgets) required to substantively update the Bayesian priors of the core claims.

- Theory thesis: The 'techno-feudalism' thesis—that digital platforms have replaced capitalist profit-making with feudal rent extraction—reflects analytical weakness on the left rather than genuine systemic transformation. Tech giants are better understood as capitalist producers making massive investments in production, not as parasitic feudal rentiers. The thesis arises from unresolved tensions in Marxist debates (Brenner vs. Wallerstein) about exploitation versus expropriation, and from frameworks ('user-ism,' cognitive capitalism) that misidentify the sources of tech-company revenues. A more expansive conception of capitalism that integrates both exploitation and expropriation eliminates the need to invoke feudalism.
