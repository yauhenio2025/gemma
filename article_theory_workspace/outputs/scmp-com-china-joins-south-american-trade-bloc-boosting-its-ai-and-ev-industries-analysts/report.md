## Bottom Line

The article describes China's acquisition of observer status in the Andean Community to secure critical minerals (lithium, copper, gold) for its AI and EV industries. While it is a news report on diplomacy rather than an economic treatise, it provides illustrative evidence for several core claims of the theory. Specifically, it concretizes the state's constitutive role in high-tech production (C3) by showing how state-level diplomacy is used to secure the material foundations of AI. It also illustrates the ongoing role of expropriation and resource extraction from the Global South (C5, C6) as a necessary component of high-tech capitalist accumulation, challenging the 'techno-feudalism' narrative that AI and digital platforms are purely intangible, rent-seeking entities detached from physical production and state power.

- Theory thesis: The 'techno-feudalism' thesis—that digital platforms have replaced capitalist profit-making with feudal rent extraction—reflects analytical weakness on the left rather than genuine systemic transformation. Tech giants are better understood as capitalist producers making massive investments in production, not as parasitic feudal rentiers. The thesis arises from unresolved tensions in Marxist debates (Brenner vs. Wallerstein) about exploitation versus expropriation, and from frameworks ('user-ism,' cognitive capitalism) that misidentify the sources of tech-company revenues. A more expansive conception of capitalism that integrates both exploitation and expropriation eliminates the need to invoke feudalism.
