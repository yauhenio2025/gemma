## Bottom Line

The article provides illustrative evidence of the capital-intensive nature of the AI economy and the state's constitutive role in building digital infrastructure, which aligns with the theory's critique of the 'techno-feudalism' thesis. Specifically, it describes China's massive state-led investment in data centers and chips (C1) and the creation of a national computing network to coordinate resources (C3). While the investment is state-led rather than private, it serves as a useful counter-example to the notion that digital platforms are 'passive rentiers' who do not invest in production. However, as a news report of policy announcements, it lacks the structured economic data or regulatory findings required to be classified as 'relevant' (direct evidence).

- Summary: China is implementing a national computing network to optimize the resource allocation of computing power across provinces, aiming to use AI as a primary economic growth engine to potentially add trillions of yuan to GDP by 2035.
- Main claims: AI is projected to contribute over 11 trillion yuan to China's GDP by 2035, A national computing network is designed to match business demand with underused resources across regions, China is pursuing self-reliance in chips and data centers to counter US export controls

- Counter-audit verdict: marginal
- Theory thesis: The 'techno-feudalism' thesis—that digital platforms have replaced capitalist profit-making with feudal rent extraction—reflects analytical weakness on the left rather than genuine systemic transformation. Tech giants are better understood as capitalist producers making massive investments in production, not as parasitic feudal rentiers. The thesis arises from unresolved tensions in Marxist debates (Brenner vs. Wallerstein) about exploitation versus expropriation, and from frameworks ('user-ism,' cognitive capitalism) that misidentify the sources of tech-company revenues. A more expansive conception of capitalism that integrates both exploitation and expropriation eliminates the need to invoke feudalism.
