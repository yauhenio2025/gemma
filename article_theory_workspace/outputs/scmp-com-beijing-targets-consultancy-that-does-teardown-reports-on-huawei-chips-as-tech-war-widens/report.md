## Bottom Line

The article describes the Chinese government's blacklisting of TechInsights, a semiconductor consultancy, in retaliation for reports on Huawei's chip procurement and SMIC's manufacturing capabilities. This is marginally relevant as it provides a concrete illustration of the state's constitutive role in the tech economy (C3) and the geopolitical competition over high-end semiconductor production (T4). It demonstrates that tech power is not 'parcelized' among private platforms but is deeply embedded in state security apparatuses and trade policy. However, it is only illustrative; it provides no systematic data on capital expenditure or revenue models that would allow it to adjudicate the rent-vs-profit debate or the structural nature of tech accumulation.

- Theory thesis: The 'techno-feudalism' thesis—that digital platforms have replaced capitalist profit-making with feudal rent extraction—reflects analytical weakness on the left rather than genuine systemic transformation. Tech giants are better understood as capitalist producers making massive investments in production, not as parasitic feudal rentiers. The thesis arises from unresolved tensions in Marxist debates (Brenner vs. Wallerstein) about exploitation versus expropriation, and from frameworks ('user-ism,' cognitive capitalism) that misidentify the sources of tech-company revenues. A more expansive conception of capitalism that integrates both exploitation and expropriation eliminates the need to invoke feudalism.
