## Bottom Line

The article reports on China's use of export controls on rare earth materials essential for advanced semiconductor and AI production. This is marginally relevant as it provides a concrete illustration of the state's constitutive role in the high-tech economy (C3), demonstrating that state power—via trade policy and resource control—actively shapes the competitive landscape of the 'chip war.' It also illustrates the theory's argument that tech dominance is tied to material resource control and state-led industrial strategy (C5/C6), rather than solely to 'digital rent' or 'platform power.' However, as a news report on a specific policy action, it lacks the systematic financial data or structural economic analysis required to adjudicate the rent-vs-profit debate or the systemic nature of accumulation.

- Theory thesis: The 'techno-feudalism' thesis—that digital platforms have replaced capitalist profit-making with feudal rent extraction—reflects analytical weakness on the left rather than genuine systemic transformation. Tech giants are better understood as capitalist producers making massive investments in production, not as parasitic feudal rentiers. The thesis arises from unresolved tensions in Marxist debates (Brenner vs. Wallerstein) about exploitation versus expropriation, and from frameworks ('user-ism,' cognitive capitalism) that misidentify the sources of tech-company revenues. A more expansive conception of capitalism that integrates both exploitation and expropriation eliminates the need to invoke feudalism.
